## 1. Voice guide (one page)

### The reconciliation
The mock speaks in a plain studio voice. The manifesto speaks in a dense origin voice.
Both are Heliacon. They are not rivals, they are two altitudes.

- **Surface (what a buyer meets first): plain studio voice.** Legible buyer outcomes.
  Navigate uncertainty, build with confidence, be found and trusted. No invented category
  at the door. This is the hero, the what-we-do list, the work teaser, the contact page.
- **Depth (what a buyer finds when they lean in): the origin-first method and the
  sovereignty spine.** One canonical source, provenance by default, you own your origin,
  only exit is sovereignty. This lives in About > approach and values, in Work > case study
  zero, in the Journal, in Services. It is the method under the promise, not the opener.

The move: **lead with the outcome, earn the right to the method, then prove it.** A reader
should be able to stop after the hero and still know what we sell. A reader who keeps going
should hit the harder, truer, un-copyable idea. Never open on "origin-first". Reach it as the
answer to "how", not as the headline.

### The through-line that connects both altitudes
Receipts. The plain voice promises clarity. The deep voice proves it is architectural, not
slogan. Both are held together by the same habit: **we show, we do not claim.** The audience
has engineering credibility and punishes hype. One inspectable fact beats ten adjectives.

### Do
- Short declaratives. One idea per sentence.
- Concrete nouns and verbs. "Serves JSON to an agent" beats "leverages AI".
- Outcomes a buyer recognises: found, cited, trusted, invoked, owned.
- Receipts inline: link the MCP endpoint, the /ask citation, the source repo.
- British English. Organise, colour, licence, optimise, analyse.
- "We" for the firm outward. On the About page, be honest it is Pete-led and deliberately small.

### Don't
- No Oxford comma. No em dash (hyphen, or split the sentence).
- No slop nouns: "systems that scale", "meaningful outcomes", "teams who see further",
  "solutions", "synergy", "leverage", "cutting-edge", "seamless".
- No brackets. No scare quotes. Rarely hyphenate compounds.
- No invented proof. No client counts, no year counts, no named case studies until real.
- Do not front the invented category. "Origin-first" is a method word, not a hero word.
- Do not overclaim the spine: exit rights stop extractive capture, they do not make software
  improve on its own. Precision survives a technical audience, slogans do not.

### Map to STYLE.md
Every rule above is `STYLE.md` applied. The one addition this spec makes is the **altitude
model**: STYLE.md governs the sentence, this governs where on the page each altitude appears.

- F2. Journal article title: **"Optimizing"** ("Edge SEO: Optimizing Beyond the Traditional
  Web") → **Optimising**.
- F3. Journal article title: **"Optimization"** ("Agent to Agent Optimization (A2AO)") →
  **Optimisation**. Also the bracketed acronym breaches the no-brackets rule; render as
  "Agent to Agent Optimisation, A2AO" or drop the acronym.
- (Note: the palette board already reads "COLOUR PALETTES", British. Good, leave it.)

**Em dash → hyphen or split**
- F4. Article body: "understand the full system**—**end to end" → "understand the full system,
  end to end" or a full stop.
- F5. Article body: "Routing isn't just infrastructure**—**it's a product decision" → split:
  "Routing isn't just infrastructure. It's a product decision."
- F6. Sweep all article/pull-quote copy for the long dash before build. The mock uses it freely.

**Unsubstantiated claims → fix or mark [NEEDS PETE]**

  Case Study" links, when no case study exists and client work is "underway, not claimed".
